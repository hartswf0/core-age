CORE-AGE: The Genealogy of a Word

The Lineage:
PIE *kerd- (Heart) → Latin cor → Vulgar Latin *corāticum → Old French corage → English Courage

The word courage traces its lineage through a rich etymological path spanning from ancient Proto-Indo-European languages to modern English, revealing a profound semantic transformation from the physical organ to abstract moral fortitude.

Proto-Indo-European Origins

The deepest root of courage reaches back to the Proto-Indo-European (PIE) root *kerd- (or *ḱḗr), meaning "heart." This ancient root is remarkably productive across Indo-European languages, manifesting in cognate terms for the heart across virtually all IE language families: Latin cor, Greek kardía, Old Irish cride, Russian serdce, German Herz, and Old English heorte. The PIE root also extended into the semantic field of belief and commitment, giving rise to words like credo (I believe), credit, and accord—all conceptually linking inner conviction to the metaphorical heart.

Latin Development

By classical Latin, cor ("heart") had become the standard term for the physical organ while simultaneously functioning as a metaphorical locus for emotions and internal states. The Romance languages inherited this core term: Spanish corazón, Italian cuore, French cœur, and Portuguese coração all descend directly from Latin cor.

The decisive etymological innovation occurred in Vulgar Latin (the vernacular language of common people in the Roman provinces), where *corāticum was formed by combining cor ("heart") with the noun-forming suffix -āticum (or -atĭcus). This Vulgar Latin derivative was specifically crafted to refer to the metaphorical, emotional heart—one's innermost feelings and temperament—rather than the physical organ. From Vulgar Latin *corāticum emerged the Romance language descendants: Old French corage (12th century), Italian coraggio, Spanish coraje, Catalan coratge, and Occitan coratge.

Old French and Medieval Development

The Old French corage (from approximately the 12th century onward) meant "heart, innermost feelings, temper," preserving the Vulgar Latin semantics but beginning a gradual process of narrowing and specialization. Importantly, at this stage in Old French and early English, the word retained much broader semantic scope than its modern meaning. It referred generally to "what is in one's mind or thoughts"—encompassing not only bravery but also emotions more broadly: wrath, pride, confidence, lustiness, and any inclination of the heart.

In Middle English (circa 1300 onwards), the English word corage was adopted directly from Old French, entering the language around the end of the 13th century. At this point, English poets and writers employed the word in formulaic phrases that reveal its semantic flexibility: bold corage ("brave heart"), careful corage ("sad heart" or "anxious heart"), fre corage ("free will"), and wikked corage ("evil heart"). These collocations demonstrate that corage served as a general term for emotional or dispositional states of being, which could be qualified by adjectives to specify their particular character.
